## Why This Portfolio?

This designer has excellent balance between showing process and outcomes. Their writing is clear, benefit-focused, and uses strong verbs. They work in healthcare and fintech - similar domains to mine.

---

## Project Title Examples

### Examples I Like:

1. "Reducing medication errors through better design"
   - *Why it works:* Starts with the outcome/benefit, clear domain signal
   - *Pattern:* Outcome + method

2. "Building trust in digital banking: A mobile-first redesign"
   - *Why it works:* Emotional benefit + context, hints at constraint (mobile-first)
   - *Pattern:* Benefit + domain + approach

3. "Making complex financial data accessible to first-time investors"
   - *Why it works:* Shows transformation, clear audience, inclusive language
   - *Pattern:* Transformation + user group

### Overall Pattern:
Usually leads with the impact or transformation, includes domain/context, and hints at the user or constraint.

## About Page Analysis

### Hero Statement:
"I'm a product designer who makes complex healthcare systems work for the people who use them."

**Why it works:**
- Clear role (product designer)
- Domain signal (healthcare)
- Benefit/outcome focus (makes systems work for people)
- Simple, direct language

### Full Bio Approach:
- Length: 2 short paragraphs (~100 words total)
- Structure: Impact-focused opening → domains/expertise → current work
- Person: First person "I" throughout

### What I Like:
- Doesn't list every tool or skill
- Focuses on impact and approach rather than credentials
- Conversational but professional
- Specific about domains (not "various industries")

## Case Study Writing Style

### Section Headers They Use:
- Context
- The Challenge
- My Approach
- Key Solutions
- Impact

**What I like about these headers:**
- Active language ("My Approach" not "Process")
- Outcome-focused ("Impact" not "Results")
- Clear and scannable

### Project Introduction Example:
"Hospital staff were spending 15 minutes per patient just navigating the scheduling system. I led the product design to streamline this workflow, reducing scheduling time by 60% and improving staff satisfaction."

**Why it works:**
- Opens with the problem (concrete, measurable)
- States role and action clearly
- Closes with quantified outcomes
- One focused paragraph

### Process Description Example:
"I started with contextual inquiry, shadowing nurses during their shifts to understand the real workflow - not just what the documentation said. This revealed that the biggest friction wasn't in the UI itself, but in how the system forced users to switch contexts constantly."

**Why it works:**
- Explains the method (contextual inquiry)
- Shows thinking beyond surface-level (real vs. documented workflow)
- Reveals insight gained
- Connects research to design decisions

## Image Captions

### Examples:

1. "Early wireframes testing different approaches to medication lists. The left version prioritized recency; the right prioritized clinical risk level."
   - *Why it works:* States what it is + what's being tested + the specific variable

2. "Usability testing revealed that clinicians didn't trust the 'smart suggestions' without seeing the underlying data. We added an expandable detail view."
   - *Why it works:* Research insight + design response, tells a story

### Pattern:
Always explains the "why" or "what was learned," not just "what is shown." Links images to decisions.

## Overall Takeaways

**What to emulate:**
1. Lead with impact/outcomes in both titles and content
2. Use specific examples and metrics rather than generalizations
3. Balance process explanation with insights gained

**What NOT to do:**
1. Don't be overly technical - balance domain language with accessibility
2. Don't list every tool used - focus on thinking and outcomes

**Key lesson from this portfolio:**
Every piece of content connects design decisions to user outcomes. Nothing is just "what I did" - it's always "what I did and why it mattered."
